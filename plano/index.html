Initial Inspection: The Foundation of Our Strategy

The first and most critical step in our termite control process is a thorough inspection of your property. Termites can be elusive, and identifying the species, the extent of the infestation, and the entry points is essential for developing an effective treatment plan. Our experienced technicians conduct a detailed examination, looking for common signs of termite activity.

What Our Inspection Includes:

  • Exterior Assessment: We examine the perimeter of your home, including the foundation, siding, eaves, and crawl spaces, searching for mud tubes, damaged wood, and other indicators of termite presence. We also assess potential entry points and conducive conditions, such as wood-to-soil contact or excessive moisture.

  • Interior Assessment: Inside your home, we inspect areas where termites are commonly found, such as basements, attics, walls, and areas around plumbing. We look for visible signs of damage, listen for clicking sounds, and may use specialized tools, like moisture meters, to identify areas of concern.

  • Identifying Termite Species: Different termite species, such as subterranean termites, drywood termites, and dampwood termites, require different treatment approaches. Our technicians are skilled at identifying the specific type of termite infesting your property to ensure the most effective control strategy is implemented.

This detailed inspection allows us to create a customized treatment plan tailored to the specific needs of your property and the type of termite infestation you're facing. We believe in transparent communication, and our technician will thoroughly explain their findings and proposed treatment options, answering any questions you may have.

Tailored Treatment Plans: Precision and Effectiveness

Based on the findings of our inspection, we develop a personalized treatment plan designed to eliminate the current infestation and prevent future activity. Our plans are not generic; they are specifically crafted for your home, considering its unique structure, the type of termites present, and your preferences.

Common Treatment Methods We Utilize:

  • Liquid Termite Barriers: This is often a highly effective method for controlling subterranean termites. We apply a liquid termiticide to the soil around the perimeter of your home, creating a barrier that termites cannot penetrate or are eliminated upon contact. This treatment is long-lasting and provides significant protection.

  • Termite Baiting Systems: Baiting systems involve placing bait stations strategically around your property. These stations contain cellulose mixed with a slow-acting termiticide. Termites consume the bait and carry it back to the colony, effectively eliminating the entire colony over time. This is an environmentally friendly option and is often used in conjunction with other treatments.

  • Direct Application to Infested Areas: For localized infestations, such as drywood termites, we may use direct application methods, injecting termiticide directly into the infested wood or structures. This targets the active termites within the wood itself.

  • Fumigation: In severe cases of drywood or other localized termite infestations throughout the entire structure, fumigation may be necessary. This involves tenting the structure and introducing a gas that penetrates all areas to eliminate termites hiding within the wood. This is a highly effective method for widespread infestations.

Our technicians are highly trained in the application of all these treatment methods, ensuring they are performed safely and effectively. We use professional-grade products that are registered and approved for use, prioritizing the safety of your family and pets.

Recognizing the Signs: Do You Need Termite Control?

Termites are often called 'silent destroyers' because they can cause significant damage without immediately obvious signs. However, by knowing what to look for, you can catch an infestation early and prevent extensive, costly damage. Don't ignore the subtle indicators; prompt action is crucial when dealing with termites. Being vigilant and aware of these signs is the first step in protecting your property.

Finding Mud Tubes

Subterranean termites build mud tubes, also known as 'shelter tubes,' to travel between their underground colonies and their food source (your home's wood). These tubes are usually about the width of a pencil and are found on foundation walls, piers, floor joists, or even inside walls. They protect the termites from predators and dry environments as they move.

If you discover mud tubes on or around your home's foundation, it's a strong indication of active subterranean termites. Disturbing a tube and seeing termites inside confirms their presence. Don't delay in contacting a professional for an inspection if you find these tubes. Early detection can prevent thousands of dollars in repairs.

Discarded Termite Wings

Swarming is the natural reproductive stage of termites. During swarming, winged reproductive termites, called 'swarmers' or 'alates,' emerge from the colony to mate and establish new colonies. After swarming, they shed their wings, often near windows, doors, or light sources where they exit the structure.

Finding small piles of discarded wings, especially near sources of light, is a definitive sign of a recent termite swarm. While the swarmers may be gone, this indicates a mature and established colony is likely present nearby. It's a clear signal that you need professional termite control services immediately.

Damaged or Blistered Wood

Termites eat wood from the inside out, often leaving a thin veneer of wood or paint on the surface. This can make the damage appear as blistering or sagging in wooden structures. Tapping on infested wood may reveal a hollow sound.

Inspect wooden structures in and around your home, such as door frames, window frames, baseboards, and floors, for any signs of damage, blistering, or discoloration. Soft or easily punctured wood can also be a sign. Termite damage often looks different from water damage, though both can cause wood to distort or discolor. A professional can distinguish between the two.

Frass or Termite Droppings

Drywood termites, which don't need contact with soil, sometimes push their fecal pellets, known as 'frass,' out of small holes in the wood they are infesting. Frass are tiny, hexagonal pellets that can collect in small piles near the infested area.

If you notice small, grainy piles of material that resemble sawdust or tiny pellets near wooden items, it could be frass from drywood termites. The color of the frass often matches the color of the wood they are consuming. This is a clear indicator of a drywood termite infestation and requires specific treatment methods.

Clicking Sounds Within Walls

Termite workers, particularly soldiers, can sometimes be heard making soft clicking or tapping sounds within infested walls. This sound is often produced when they are disturbed or warning the colony of danger. While not always easy to hear, it can be a sign of active termites.

Listen closely to your walls, especially in quiet areas of your home. If you hear faint clicking or tapping sounds, it could be a sign of termites. A more obvious sign might be seeing termites, which resemble ants but have straight antennae and a broad waist. Ants, conversely, have elbowed antennae and a pinched waist.

Stuck Windows or Doors

As termites consume wood, they can cause it to warp and swell. This can make windows and doors difficult to open or close. While this can also be caused by humidity, if it's accompanied by other signs of termites, it's worth investigating.

If you notice windows or doors that suddenly become difficult to operate or appear swollen, it might be due to termite damage. Don't dismiss these issues as just old house problems. They could be indicators of a hidden termite infestation causing structural damage. Professional inspection is recommended to determine the cause.

Pinpoint Holes in Drywall or Plaster

Termites can sometimes make tiny holes in drywall or plaster surfaces as they tunnel within the walls. These holes are often small and may not be immediately noticeable, but they can be a sign of termites inside your walls.

Inspect your walls for any small, inexplicable holes. While other pests or minor damage could cause these, if you see other signs of termites, these pinpoint holes could be another indicator of an active infestation. These small openings are sometimes used by termites to expel frass or as entry/exit points.

Hollow Sounding Wood

As termites consume the interior of wooden beams, floors, or walls, the wood becomes hollow. Tapping on the surface of the wood can produce a hollow sound, indicating that the inner structure has been eaten away.

Gently tap on wooden areas you suspect may be infested. Solid wood will produce a firm sound, while termite-damaged wood will sound hollow. This is a tell-tale sign of significant structural damage and requires immediate attention from a professional termite control service.

Sagging Floors or Ceilings

In severe cases of infestation, the structural integrity of your home can be compromised. As termites eat through supporting beams and joists, it can lead to sagging floors or ceilings. This is a critical sign that the infestation is advanced and requires urgent intervention.

Visible sagging in floors or ceilings is a serious indicator of extensive termite damage. This level of damage often means the infestation has been active for a considerable time and requires significant repairs in addition to termite eradication. Don't ignore these signs; they signal potential structural failure.

Musty Odor

In some cases, a musty or moldy odor may indicate the presence of termites, particularly subterranean termites. These termites require high levels of moisture, and the presence of moisture and the termites themselves can create a distinctive smell, often akin to mildew.

If you notice an unexplained musty odor in enclosed spaces like basements or crawl spaces, especially if accompanied by other signs, it could be related to a termite infestation. While moisture issues can cause a musty smell on their own, termites often contribute to these conditions. A professional can assess the source of the odor.
